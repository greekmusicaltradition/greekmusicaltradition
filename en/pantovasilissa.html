A Living Memorial Through Song

At the heart of Rafina stands the Church of Panagia Pantovasilissa, built in the mid-20th century by refugees from Triglia (modern-day Tirilye), Asia Minor. This sacred space serves as a living memorial to their origins and the rich Byzantine heritage they carried across the Aegean Sea.

Our choir meets in this welcoming spirit, keeping alive the richness of Greek traditional music through authentic songs from all corners of Greece. The rehearsals, conducted in Greek, bring together melodies that tell the stories of our people, nurturing both musical skill and cultural connection.

The Sacred Heritage of Triglia

The Church of Panagia Pantovasilissa carries forward the legacy of the original Byzantine church in Triglia, Asia Minor, which bore the same sacred name. The miraculous icon of Panagia Pantovasilissa, believed to be painted by Saint Luke the Evangelist, was saved from the destruction of Constantinople in 1453 and found its way to Triglia's shores.

For centuries, this holy icon blessed the faithful community of Triglia until 1922, when the Asia Minor Catastrophe forced over one million Greek Orthodox Christians to flee their ancestral homes. The refugees who settled in Rafina brought with them not only the memory of their beloved church but also their rich musical traditions.

Today, our choir continues this unbroken chain of faith and culture, singing the same traditional songs that echoed through the hills of Triglia and all regions of Greece.

The Sacred Icon of Panagia Pantovasilissa

The name "Pantovasilissa" means "Queen of All" and represents the Theotokos (Mother of God) in her role as sovereign protector. According to tradition, this holy icon was one of four painted by Saint Luke the Evangelist and was originally housed in the Hagia Sophia in Constantinople.

When Constantinople fell in 1453, the sacred icons were cast into the sea to prevent their desecration. The Pantovasilissa icon miraculously appeared on the shores of Triglia, where it blessed the community for centuries with numerous recorded miracles and wonders.

Today, the original icon is preserved in the Byzantine Museum of Athens, while a faithful copy continues to bless our church community in Rafina, connecting us to this unbroken chain of faith and devotion.
